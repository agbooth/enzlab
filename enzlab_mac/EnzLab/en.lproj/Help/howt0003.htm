Planning your experimental strategy

General strategy

You must think carefully about

  • what you want to know
  • what you already know - about enzymes in general and about your particular enzyme

Remind yourself how the rate of an enzyme-catalysed reaction depends on

  • the substrate concentration
  • the enzyme concentration

You may assume your enzyme obeys Michaelis-Menten kinetics. (To remind yourself, sketch graphs of Vo against S and Vo against E.)

Aims of your study

  • What is the enzyme's pH optimum?
  • What effect does pH have on Km and Vmax?
  • Does azide inhibit substrate binding, the catalytic process, or both?
  • What is the dissociation constant for the enzyme-inhibitor complex?
  • How precise are your measurements of the reaction rate?

There is no single correct way to answer these questions. You must choose your own preferred strategy.

Points to bear in mind

  • How far can you get without knowing the precision of your measurements? (You have already used the assay procedure and so must have some confidence in it.)
  • Your studies of pH will be much helped if you have at least an estimate of Km at a standard pH.
  • Your studies of the inhibitor will be much helped if you have at least some information obtained in the absence of inhibitor - the more the better.
  • You have a limited supply of enzyme.

Also bear in mind that it is efficient, in the laboratory, to make few changes to your experimental conditions on a given day. A series of kinetic runs in which you change all parameters (vol, pH, S, I) is more cumbersome than a series in which you change only one.
